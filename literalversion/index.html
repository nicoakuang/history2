Handwritten Draft of "Declaration of the Chinese Government" with Former President Chiang Kai-shek's Annotations
先總統蔣介石先生手批「中國政府宣言」手稿

(Formerly Collected by Wang Liangchou (王亮疇, Wáng Liàngchóu))

Background Context: The Global Situation in 1940

On June 14, 1940, German forces broke through France's Maginot Line and occupied Paris. On June 22, the French government and the German government signed an armistice agreement. On June 20, France, acting under Japanese instructions, closed the Indochina Railway (滇越鐵路, Diānyuè Tiělù) to traffic with China. The "Declaration of the Chinese Government" was issued precisely in response to this matter. On the 24th, Japan (日本, Rìběn) further demanded that Britain close the Burma Road (滇緬公路, Diānyuǎn Gōnglù) to traffic with China. The British government subsequently announced that, effective July 18, a three-month embargo would be implemented, thereby almost completely severing China's external communication.

The Declaration of the Chinese Government

French Indochina (法屬越南, Fǎshǔ Yuènán), in terms of geography and communication, has always maintained a close relationship. From the perspective of commercial and balanced economic needs, mutually beneficial interests have been enjoyed for many years; thus, the two are inseparable. Indochina especially serves as an international communication route for our country, bearing immense significance for our country's prosperity and security.

China and France have signed several treaties concerning French Indochina. The most recent is the "Sino-French Convention on the Relationship between Indochina and China's Border Provinces (中法規定越南及中國邊省關係專約, Zhōngfǎ Guīdìng Yuènán jí Zhōngguó Biānsheng Guānxì Zhuānyuē)," concluded on May 16, 1930, and effective July 22, 1935. According to the provisions of this treaty, France permits various goods, including military equipment and armaments, to pass through Indochina. Furthermore, Article Six explicitly stipulates: "All military supplies and armaments transported by the Chinese government, when passing through Tonkin (東京, Dōngjīng) (Indochina), shall be exempt from any duties or taxes."

Resolutions from previous sessions of the League of Nations (國際聯合會, Guójì Liánhéhuì) stipulate that all member states, including France, should avoid any actions that could diminish China's capacity for resistance and should consider individually assisting China.

The Chinese government, in view of France's aforementioned specific obligations, has the right to demand that France fulfill its obligations and maintain the international communication route through Indochina. However, in recent years, considering France's difficult circumstances, China has therefore empathized with the hardships of the French government.

Regarding military equipment and armaments through Indochina, China did not demand passage. This was China's most friendly gesture and also its greatest forbearance.

Since the Chinese government has already accommodated France's wishes and shown utmost humility, it is truly baffling that the French government now responds by prohibiting the passage of various goods through Indochina, effectively blockading China's crucial entry and exit routes, even severing our international communication.

The impact of this ban is extremely severe. The Chinese government cannot but regard this as a most unfriendly act, which is unacceptable to the Chinese people. For France to impose a blockade upon its friendly ally is already utterly unreasonable, let alone considering its obligations under treaties and the League of Nations.

The French government's sudden change in attitude this time will inevitably encourage disruptors of peace in the Far East. Regarding this matter, the Chinese government cannot but take appropriate measures, for it must be known that if Japan engages in any military aggression or action in any region of Asia or the Pacific, regardless of its form, our Chinese government deeply believes that its sole purpose is to annihilate China's survival and independence. Therefore, if Japan takes any military action in various places in Asia and the Pacific, and if it obstructs China's legitimate rights—especially those related to China's economic communication—China, for the sake of its survival, independence, and preservation of rights, cannot but be forced by Japan to adopt necessary measures under such circumstances. Hence, this declaration.

The Lushan Speech (1937)

On July 7, 1937 (the 26th year of the Republic of China), the Marco Polo Bridge Incident (盧溝橋事變, Lúgōuqiao Shìbiàn / 七七事變, Qīqī Shìbiàn) erupted. On July 17, Former President Chiang Kai-shek (先總統蔣介石, Xiān Zǒngtǒng Jiǎng Jièshí) delivered a speech at Lushan (廬山, Lúshān), stating:

"We know that once the nation is compelled to engage in war, there is no hope of escaping sacrifice. If war breaks out, then regardless of north or south, young or old, everyone has the responsibility to defend their homeland and resist aggression. Everyone must resolve to sacrifice everything."

— Chiang Kai-shek, Lushan Speech, July 17, 1937

This speech, a profound call to total national resistance, resonated through China as it faced the escalating full-scale invasion by Imperial Japan.

Key Terms & Glossary

French Indochina (法屬越南 / Fǎshǔ Yuènán)

►

A federation of French colonial possessions in Southeast Asia (comprising Tonkin, Annam, Cochinchina, Cambodia, and Laos). During World War II, its railway was crucial for Chinese supply lines, leading to its closure under Japanese pressure.

Indochina Railway (滇越鐵路 / Diānyuè Tiělù)

►

A railway connecting Kunming in Yunnan, China, with Haiphong in French Indochina. It served as a vital supply route for China during the early stages of the Second Sino-Japanese War until its closure in June 1940.

Burma Road (滇緬公路 / Diānyuǎn Gōnglù)

►

A strategic highway linking Burma (Myanmar) with China's Yunnan province. It became China's last major lifeline for war supplies after the Indochina Railway was closed, until it too was temporarily shut by Britain in July 1940 under Japanese pressure.

Vichy France (維琪法國 / Wéiqí Fǎguó)

►

The French State, led by Marshal Philippe Pétain, during World War II from 1940 to 1944. It was a German collaborationist regime that, under Japanese pressure, agreed to close critical supply routes to China.

League of Nations (國際聯合會 / Guójì Liánhéhuì)

►

An intergovernmental organization founded in 1920 to promote world peace. China frequently cited its resolutions against Japanese aggression, particularly concerning non-recognition of gains acquired by force.

Marco Polo Bridge Incident (盧溝橋事變 / Lúgōuqiao Shìbiàn, also 七七事變 / Qīqī Shìbiàn)

►

A pivotal military engagement between the Republic of China's National Revolutionary Army and the Imperial Japanese Army that occurred on July 7, 1937, near Beijing. This incident, often referred to as the July 7th Incident, is widely regarded as the full-scale start of the Second Sino-Japanese War (中日戰爭, Zhōng-Rì Zhànzhēng).
