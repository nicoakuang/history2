Handwritten Draft of "Declaration of the Chinese Government" with Former President Chiang Kai-shek's Annotations
先總統蔣介石先生手批「中國政府宣言」手稿

(Formerly Collected by Wang Liangchou (王亮疇, *Wáng Liàngchóu*))

Background: The 1940 Global Crisis and China's Isolation

The year 1940 marked a pivotal and perilous turning point in World War II, sending profound shockwaves through the embattled Republic of China (中華民國, *Zhōnghuá Mínguó*) amidst its arduous War of Resistance against Japan. On June 14, the swift advance of German forces culminated in the occupation of Paris, crippling French resistance. This led to the French government's signing of an armistice agreement with Germany on June 22, formally ushering in the era of Vichy France. This collaborationist regime, now under German influence, inadvertently created a dangerous geopolitical vacuum in Asia, one that Japanese imperialists were eager to exploit.

Seizing upon France's weakened state, Japan (日本, *Rìběn*) swiftly moved to sever China's vital supply lines. Just six days after the fall of Paris, on June 20, Japan pressured the newly established Vichy French administration to close the crucial Indochina Railway (滇越鐵路, *Diānyuè Tiělù*). This railway, a lifeline connecting Kunming to Haiphong, had been instrumental in transporting essential war materials. Its closure severely hampered China's resistance efforts, virtually choking off a primary external communication route.

The pressure intensified days later when Japan demanded that Britain, grappling with its own existential struggle in Europe, also close the Burma Road (滇緬公路, *Diānyuǎn Gōnglù*). This road was China's last remaining major conduit for international supplies. Facing immense pressure, the British government reluctantly announced on July 18 the implementation of a three-month embargo. With both arteries of aid severed, the Republic of China found its external communications almost entirely cut off, facing isolation at a critical juncture. It was against this backdrop of escalating global conflict and profound diplomatic betrayal that the Republic of China government issued its formal statement, the "Declaration of the Chinese Government," a powerful testament to its unyielding resolve.

The Declaration of the Chinese Government

The Declaration opens by establishing the enduring bond between China and French Indochina (法屬越南, *Fǎshǔ Yuènán*), asserting their inseparable ties not only geographically but also through a history of mutually beneficial commercial and economic exchanges. Critically, Indochina is highlighted as an international communication route of paramount importance to China's prosperity and security, underscoring its strategic value to the nation's survival.

This strategic relationship was formally enshrined in a series of treaties between China and France. The most recent and relevant, the "Sino-French Convention on the Relationship between Indochina and China's Border Provinces (中法規定越南及中國邊省關係專約, *Zhōngfǎ Guīdìng Yuènán jí Zhōngguó Biānsheng Guānxì Zhuānyuē*)," signed on May 16, 1930, and effective July 22, 1935, explicitly outlined these rights. Its Article Six was particularly vital, unequivocally stipulating that France was bound to permit the passage of all types of goods through Indochina, including military equipment and armaments transported by the Chinese government through Tonkin (東京, *Dōngjīng*), free from any duties or taxes. This treaty, therefore, formed a clear legal basis for China's expectation of continued, unhindered transit of essential supplies.

Furthermore, the Declaration referenced the resolutions from previous sessions of the League of Nations (國際聯合會, *Guójì Liánhéhuì*). These international mandates stipulated that all member states, including France, were obligated to refrain from any actions that could diminish China's capacity for resistance against aggression, and were indeed urged to consider providing individual assistance to China.

In light of these clear obligations, the Chinese government affirmed its rightful demand for France to uphold its duties and maintain the integrity of the international communication route through Indochina. Yet, China revealed its prior diplomatic patience: for years, understanding France's domestic difficulties, China had chosen to empathize with the French government's hardships and had, as a gesture of goodwill, refrained from strictly demanding the passage of military equipment and armaments. This, the Declaration stated, represented China's most friendly gesture and greatest act of forbearance.

However, this spirit of accommodation was met with a shocking betrayal. Despite China's prior humility and concessions, the French government now inexplicably responded by prohibiting the passage of various goods through Indochina, effectively blockading China's crucial entry and exit routes. This action amounted to nothing less than the severing of China's vital international communication. The impact of this ban was deemed extremely severe, a direct threat to China's ability to resist. The Chinese government declared that it could not but view this as a most unfriendly act, utterly unacceptable to the Chinese people. For France to impose such a blockade upon a friendly ally, while simultaneously disregarding its solemn obligations under international treaties and League of Nations resolutions, was deemed to be entirely without justification.

The Declaration concluded with a stark warning: this sudden and inexplicable shift in French policy would inevitably embolden and encourage those seeking to disrupt peace in the Far East. The Chinese government, therefore, asserted its unequivocal right to take appropriate measures in response. It unequivocally stated its profound belief that Japan's singular and overarching purpose, should it engage in any military aggression within Asia or the Pacific, regardless of its form or location, was to annihilate China's very survival and independence. Thus, the Declaration firmly asserted that if Japan's actions were to obstruct China's legitimate rights—especially those intrinsically linked to its economic communication—China would be left with no choice but to adopt all necessary measures forced upon it by such circumstances, for the sake of its national survival, independence, and the preservation of its fundamental rights. This declaration was a clear statement of China's unwavering determination to defend itself at all costs.

The Lushan Speech: A Call to Total Resistance (1937)

The "Declaration of the Chinese Government" in 1940 echoed a spirit of unyielding resistance articulated earlier by Former President Chiang Kai-shek. On July 7, 1937, the pivotal Marco Polo Bridge Incident (盧溝橋事變, *Lúgōuqiao Shìbiàn* / 七七事變, *Qīqī Shìbiàn*) erupted near Beijing, marking the full-scale outbreak of the Second Sino-Japanese War (中日戰爭, *Zhōng-Rì Zhànzhēng*). Ten days later, on July 17, Chiang delivered a pivotal address at Lushan (廬山, *Lúshān*), outlining China's resolve to fight.

"We know that once the nation is compelled to engage in war, there is no hope of escaping sacrifice. If war breaks out, then regardless of north or south, young or old, everyone has the responsibility to defend their homeland and resist aggression. Everyone must resolve to sacrifice everything."

— Chiang Kai-shek, Lushan Speech, July 17, 1937

This speech galvanized the nation, setting the stage for eight years of arduous struggle. The 1940 Declaration, in turn, reaffirmed China's determination to resist external pressures and maintain its sovereignty, even as the global geopolitical landscape drastically changed.

Key Terms & Glossary

French Indochina (法屬越南 / Fǎshǔ Yuènán)

►

A federation of French colonial possessions in Southeast Asia, comprising Tonkin, Annam, Cochinchina (which make up modern Vietnam), Cambodia, and Laos. During WWII, its railway was crucial for Chinese supply lines.

Indochina Railway (滇越鐵路 / Diānyuè Tiělù)

►

A railway connecting Kunming in Yunnan, China, with Haiphong in French Indochina. It was a vital supply route for China during the early stages of the Second Sino-Japanese War until its closure in June 1940.

Burma Road (滇緬公路 / Diānyuǎn Gōnglù)

►

A strategic highway linking Burma (Myanmar) with China's Yunnan province. It served as a major conduit for war supplies to China after the Indochina Railway was closed, becoming China's last lifeline until it too was temporarily shut by Britain in July 1940 under Japanese pressure.

Vichy France (維琪法國 / Wéiqí Fǎguó)

►

The French State, led by Marshal Philippe Pétain, during World War II from 1940 to 1944. It was a German collaborationist regime, which, under Japanese pressure, agreed to close critical supply routes to China.

League of Nations (國際聯合會 / Guójì Liánhéhuì)

►

An intergovernmental organization founded in 1920 as a result of the Paris Peace Conference that ended World War I. It was the first worldwide intergovernmental organization whose principal mission was to maintain world peace. China frequently cited its resolutions against Japanese aggression.

Marco Polo Bridge Incident (盧溝橋事變 / Lúgōuqiao Shìbiàn, also 七七事變 / Qīqī Shìbiàn)

►

A pivotal military engagement between the Republic of China's National Revolutionary Army and the Imperial Japanese Army that occurred on July 7, 1937, near Beijing. This incident, often referred to as the July 7th Incident, is widely regarded as the full-scale start of the Second Sino-Japanese War (中日戰爭, *Zhōng-Rì Zhànzhēng*).
